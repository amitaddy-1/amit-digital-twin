A few months ago, I had to give a forty-minute talk at a conference, and I decided, for reasons I now think of as mostly vanity, to do it entirely without notes. No slides. No speaker notes. Just me, the audience, and whatever my brain remembered to hand over on the day.

The week before, I sat in my living room and built what memory athletes call a memory palace. I walked my talk through my own apartment, room by room. The opening went into the kitchen, parked next to the gas stove. The first big idea sat on the dining table. The main story I wanted to tell lived on the sofa. The data section moved into the corner with the houseplant. Each section was anchored to a place, or an object, or a window. The location became the cue. The cue summoned the words.

And it worked. Forty minutes, no fumbling. I came home rather pleased with myself and reported the small triumph to my wife, who suggested mildly that perhaps I could now also remember to take the plastic trash out on Fridays. The memory palace, she observed, had limits.

The memory palace trick itself is ancient. The Romans used it. The Greeks before them. But the quiet engine humming underneath every memory palace ever built is one of the loveliest ideas in cognitive science, and it was written down in 1949 by a Canadian psychologist named Donald Hebb.

Hebb's idea, in his book "The Organization of Behaviour", was this: when one brain cell repeatedly helps fire another, the connection between them strengthens. Years later, the neuroscientist Carla Shatz boiled the whole thing down into the line a lot of us now know: "Cells that fire together, wire together."

That is the Hebbian Rule. Six words. Possibly the most quotable sentence in the history of the brain.

What makes it lovely is how much it explains. Pavlov's dogs salivating at a bell? Hebbian. A song from college days pulling a long-forgotten feeling back to the surface? Hebbian. The smell of agarbatti placing you instantly in your parents' living room? Also Hebbian. Every mnemonic trick the world has ever invented, including my conference memory palace, is just a deliberate effort to wire two things together so that one can later summon the other.

For decades Hebb's idea sat as a hypothesis. It became biology in 1973, when researchers discovered long-term potentiation — synapses really do get stronger when neurons co-fire. Hebb, who had been arguing for this from an armchair, was vindicated.

The more interesting second application of his rule, though, has been in artificial intelligence. In 1982, John Hopfield built a neural network where memories were stored exactly as Hebb had described, in the strengths of connections between neurons. You could feed it a partial cue and it would summon the rest. Hopfield networks were the first real "associative memory" machines, Hebbian to the core. Hopfield went on to win the Nobel Prize in Physics for this work in 2024, alongside Geoffrey Hinton. The wait was long, but the bell finally rang.

You can still see Hebb in the AI we use every day. Word embeddings cluster co-occurring concepts. Attention mechanisms strengthen the weight between tokens that keep showing up together. Large language models, at one level, are industrial-scale association. Cells that fire together, wire together — only the cells are now silicon and the firing happens at speed.

So the next time I have to give a talk without notes, I will silently thank Donald Hebb. And then I will worry. Because Hebbian learning also explains why I still remember, with painful clarity, the taste of corn patties I had at Mahabaleshwar 10 years ago and the name of the boy who sat behind me in school and kicked my bench for an entire year. Cells that fire together really do wire together. Sometimes, unfortunately, against your will.
